Artifact overview

Why this work belongs in my professional portfolio

Artificial intelligence has changed significantly since the 1950s. It developed from an abstract research question into one of the most influential technologies used today. I selected my AI and ML timeline as Artifact 1 because it demonstrates my ability to research a broad technical topic, identify the most meaningful information, organize it chronologically, and explain it visually to a professional audience.

The timeline begins with Alan Turing's work on machine intelligence and continues through the perceptron, ELIZA, expert systems, the AI winters, Deep Blue, Watson, AlexNet, AlphaGo, transformer architecture, ChatGPT, and multimodal generative AI. It also recognizes the role of organizations such as IBM, Google, DeepMind, NVIDIA, Microsoft, and OpenAI in advancing research and expanding access to AI systems.

Intended audience

Hiring managers, software professionals, classmates, instructors, and business stakeholders who want a clear introduction to the historical development of AI.

Professional purpose

To demonstrate research, technical communication, visual organization, critical thinking, and an ability to connect historical developments to modern business applications.

Selected milestones

From machine intelligence to multimodal AI

The complete visual presentation is available through the Canva link above.

1950

Alan Turing and machine intelligence

Turing's paper introduced the question of whether machines can think and proposed the imitation game as a practical test.

1958

The perceptron

Frank Rosenblatt's perceptron became an early model of machine learning inspired by the way biological neurons process signals.

1966

ELIZA

ELIZA demonstrated early natural-language interaction and showed how people could perceive simple text patterns as meaningful conversation.

1974–1980

First AI winter

Limited computing power, unrealistic promises, and disappointing results caused reduced investment and slower progress.

1980s

Expert systems

Rule-based systems brought AI into business settings by reproducing specialized decision processes in areas such as diagnosis and configuration.

1987–1993

Second AI winter

High costs, limited flexibility, and unmet expectations again reduced confidence and funding in commercial AI systems.

1997

IBM Deep Blue

Deep Blue defeated world chess champion Garry Kasparov, demonstrating the strength of specialized computing and search.

2011

IBM Watson

Watson's Jeopardy! victory showed progress in question answering, language processing, information retrieval, and probabilistic reasoning.

2012

AlexNet and deep learning

AlexNet achieved a major image-recognition breakthrough and helped establish GPUs and deep neural networks as powerful AI tools.

2016

AlphaGo

DeepMind's AlphaGo defeated Lee Sedol by combining deep learning, reinforcement learning, and tree search.

2017

Transformer architecture

The transformer enabled efficient attention-based language modeling and became the foundation for many modern generative AI systems.

2022+

Generative and multimodal AI

Large-scale systems began generating and interpreting text, images, audio, video, and code, expanding AI into everyday professional workflows.

Required reflection

Artifact 1 reflection

Why I selected this artifact

I selected the AI and ML timeline because it demonstrates several qualities that I want employers and other professionals to see in my work. It shows that I can learn an unfamiliar or fast-changing topic, identify important developments, evaluate their significance, and communicate the information in a structured format. As a software developer expanding into AI and machine learning, understanding this history gives me a stronger foundation for evaluating current tools and future trends.

What the artifact demonstrates

The artifact demonstrates research, organization, visual communication, and critical thinking. Instead of presenting AI as a smooth path of continuous progress, I included the AI winters to show that innovation often develops through cycles of expectations, limitations, learning, and renewed investment. This perspective is important in professional settings because organizations should evaluate emerging technology realistically rather than adopting it only because it is popular.

Value for my audience

The timeline is useful for a professional audience because it provides a clear overview without requiring advanced technical knowledge. A hiring manager can see my ability to explain complex ideas, while a technical reader can recognize the connections among algorithms, data, computing power, cloud platforms, and business adoption. The artifact also creates a starting point for conversations about responsible AI implementation and technology leadership.

What I learned

Working on this timeline helped me understand that AI progress depends on more than algorithms. Large datasets, GPUs, scalable cloud computing, research investment, and collaboration among universities and technology companies all contributed to recent breakthroughs. I also learned that setbacks can improve a field by forcing researchers and organizations to reconsider weak assumptions and develop more practical approaches.

How I would improve the artifact

In a future version, I would add more information about responsible AI, bias, privacy, governance, cybersecurity, and the social impact of automation. I would also include short interactive explanations, source links for every milestone, and examples of how each breakthrough affected healthcare, education, finance, retail, and software development. These additions would make the timeline more useful for both technical and nontechnical audiences.
